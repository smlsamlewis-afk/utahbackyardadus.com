How ADUs get utilities — and why it matters for price

Utility connections are the most variable line item on any ADU project. Here's how they typically work and the budget ranges to expect on a Utah build.

For most backyard ADUs, the cheapest path is to tie into the existing house's services — sharing the main electrical panel, the house's water main, and the existing sewer lateral. That's the path we recommend whenever the existing infrastructure has spare capacity.

The alternative is a separate service from the city or utility — its own electric meter, water meter, and sewer tap. That costs more upfront but lets you bill the unit independently, which matters for some rental setups.

Three things drive the final number more than anything else: distance from the house to the ADU (every foot of trench costs money), the condition and capacity of your existing service (an old 100A panel may need an upgrade), and your city's permit and impact fees (these vary widely across Utah).

⚡ Electrical

Most common: a subpanel fed from the main house panel — typically 60A or 100A — through a buried conduit. Requires a load calculation; older 100A or 125A main services often need a 200A upgrade.

Alternative: separate meter and service drop from the utility. More expensive, but fully independent billing.

Typical range: $3,000 – $10,000 (subpanel) · $8,000 – $20,000+ (separate service)

💧 Water

Most common: tee off the home's existing water supply line. Quick and affordable when the line has capacity.

Alternative: separate water meter and tap from the city main — required by some Utah municipalities, especially for ADUs intended as long-term rentals.

Typical range: $2,000 – $5,000 (shared) · $5,000 – $15,000+ (separate meter)

🚰 Sewer

Most common: tie into the existing house lateral, usually at the property-line cleanout. Gravity has to work — if the ADU sits low, you may need an ejector pump (+$1,500–$3,500).

Alternative: a new dedicated lateral to the city main. Often required if the existing lateral is undersized or in poor condition.

Typical range: $3,000 – $8,000 (shared) · $8,000 – $20,000+ (new lateral)

🔥 Gas (or All-Electric)

You can extend a new gas line from the house meter to the ADU, or skip gas entirely with a heat pump and induction range. All-electric is increasingly the cost-effective choice in Utah and qualifies for several utility rebates.

Typical range: $2,000 – $5,000 (gas line) · $0 add'l (all-electric)

Rule of thumb for budgeting: on a typical Utah lot with a short trench run (under 30 feet), shared services, and no panel upgrade needed, plan on roughly $12,000–$25,000 all-in for utilities. On a tougher site — long trench, panel upgrade, separate meters, or a new sewer tap — utilities can easily run $35,000–$60,000+. We'll give you a firm number after walking the site and pulling a load calc.
